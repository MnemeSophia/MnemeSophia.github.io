How to Play with a GM

I GM a lot of roleplay online, and have a specific vision of what roleplay between a Player and a GM should look like. As a rule, nobody does it how I want them to unless I teach them, so here's a manual of "How to Play with a GM."

Before talking about the rules and principles of playing with a GM, I'll give a simplified example.

GM: You're in a room. There's a window and a desk with a chair at it. On the desk are a few scattered pages, a jar of ink, and a quill

Player: Is there anything written on the pages?

GM: There are several pages that look like drafts of a letter to a certain "Lucille."

Player: Is the ink wet, or dry?

GM: One page near the top is still damp.

Player: I'll collect up those pages, then. What's out of the window?

GM: Looking out the window, you see the yard.

Player: Can I jump out the window?

GM: Looks like you could.

Player: Then I will.

GM: You jump out. You're in the yard now...

Now that you've seen it, let's talk.

How to Play with a GM

1) The game is a conversation.

There is very little room for even a paragraph-length reply, here. The GM might hit a paragraph or more when the scene or situation changes, but if the player is typing out lengthy, novel-style prose, we're going to mismatch. Far from frowning on one-liners, I encourage them. The game is a conversation, and conversation is a free-flowing, rapid exchange of words.

2) The game takes place in our imaginations.

This means a few things. First, it reinforces the first point. The gameplay is not in a paragraph. It's in our minds meeting via conversation. Second, in the context of a GM-and-Player conversation, it means that we have to establish a consistent imagined world between the two of us. How do we do that?

  • By asking questions.
  • By clarifying descriptions.
  • By rewinding when we have a "glitch," i.e. a moment where we failed to sync our imaginations.

3) The GM describes the world. The Player declares intent. The GM resolves actions. Repeat.

This is the basic structure of play. The conversation essentially cycles around these three steps over, and over, and over, until we finish playing. Let's break them down.

The GM describes the world. This is self-explanatory, but important. The GM does not say what your character does. Only the world around them.

The Player declares intent. Intent is not action, per se. The player describes what they want to do, what they intend to do.

The GM resolves actions. Once the player has declared their intent to do something, how that turns out is in the GM's hands. If the Player intends to jump off a building, they don't say whether they survive the fall. The result of Player action is for the GM to determine.

Repeat. When player actions are resolved, the situation updates. The GM fills the Player in on what's changed, and we do the process again.

The Role and Roll of Dice

Not every roleplay needs dice. Most don't. I sometimes use dice to maintain neutrality. It's one thing to say your character died when they jumped off a plane without a parachute. That's obvious It's another to say how a battle between your character and an equally matched opponent turns out. If I decide you win, am I making the game too easy? If I decide you lose, am I biased against you? Messy.

Solution: If I roll an even number, you win. If I roll an odd number, you lose.

Slightly weaker opponent? They win on a 1-2, you win on a 3, 4, 5, or 6.

Want to run recklessly across a rickety rope bridge after that rival treasure hunter? On a 1-out-of-6, the bridge collapses. Otherwise, you make it across.

I don't always use the same dice, or the same system, but the dice and system aren't the point. I'm simply using dice to offload decision-making to a less potentially-biased power.

Conclusion

This style is not for everyone. If you're a writer using roleplay as a way of stretching your literary muscles, you'll probably not get what you want from this.

If you find OOC chat with your partners more fun than the actual RP, this is for you.

If you are looking to play a game that's more realistic than any videogame you've played, this is for you.

If exploring a living world is as interesting or more than exploring character motives and themes, this is for you.

If you're not sure about this, I want you to try it.
